The Austrian Diet

What we eat every day leaves a significant footprint on our planet. This visual journey breaks down the Austrian food matrix: from our country's macroscopic production volumes to the hidden ecological costs of seemingly simple foods like cheese, all the way to the complex biochemical networks embedded in our food. Explore the invisible connections between consumption, nature, and biology.

The Macro balance

Austria is a country known for its wines and beer, as well as for other cuisines such as beef soup, apple strudel, and, of course, schnitzel. These are just a few among many great dishes Austrians enjoy on a day-to-day basis. However, this is only true from the perspective of the consumer, because when viewed under the lens of Austria's industry, the picture changes quite drastically.

Cheese & Ecological Cost

Creating dairy products requires a staggering amount of resources. To produce just 1 kg of hard cheese, Austrian dairies process roughly 10 liters of raw milk, leaving behind 9 liters of liquid whey and water loss.

While this physical mass balance looks straightforward, the true environmental toll remains hidden beneath the surface.

Click on "Cheese" to reveal the massive secondary flows of the ecological footprint: from greenhouse gas emissions to land use and virtual water withdrawal

The Environmental Shift

Different foods require different means of productions; our food originates from many diverse environments, with each product needing its own unique procedure before it can end up on our plate. The following visualization shows a plethora of food products and their impact on the environment.

One glance at the scatter plot, and you will quickly notice that some products have a small, far less noticeable freshwater withdrawal foodprint than, for example, cheese. This is why we have given you the option to interact with and explore this data yourself!

These are the controls:

  • Scroll to zoom: Make sure to really get in there, some products are quite hidden!
  • Reset to the default view by using the red button in the control panel on the right.

Conclusion ﹀

Our data exploration reveals that food is fundamentally interconnected, serving as a bridge between global resources and biochemistry. A simple choice like buying cheese carries a heavy ecological burden, hiding a complex footprint of land use and emissions beneath the surface. To make sustainable choices, we must understand the profound link between a food's nutritional value and its environmental cost. Every choice matters, for our bodies, and our planet.
